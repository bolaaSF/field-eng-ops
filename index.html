Two RevOps Functions — Complementary, Not Competing

Vercel has two RevOps roles. Renee Kelley (Sr. Director, Revenue Operations) reports to the CRO and owns the sales-side operating model — pipeline, forecasting, territory for quota-carrying reps. This role (VP RevOps, Field Engineering) reports to David Totten and owns the technical-side operating model — consumption tracking, cross-team handoffs, field-to-product feedback, comp design for non-quota technical teams.

The two functions share the CRM and data infrastructure but own different motions. Renee's team asks: “How do we close more deals?” This role asks: “How do we make every deal successful after it closes — and turn that success into expansion?”

In a consumption model, post-sale ops is arguably more important than pre-sale ops. Revenue isn't recognized at booking — it's recognized at consumption. The team that drives consumption IS the revenue team.

The Commercial Engine

How revenue actually flows at a consumption-based platform company — and where Field Engineering is the critical path.

What Is a Commercial Engine?

Not just a sales process. It's the complete system that turns market signals into revenue and revenue into growth signals. For consumption-based companies like Vercel, the engine looks fundamentally different from traditional SaaS — field engineering isn't downstream of sales, it is the revenue motion.

Layer 4 Goals, Comp & Investment Model How you pay people determines how they behave ›

In a consumption model, comp drives behavior more than process. If you pay SEs on bookings, they'll close deals that don't consume. If you pay CSMs on renewal, they'll sand-bag expansion.

Investment Per Account

  • Cost-to-serve by tier (SE hours, PS days, Support tickets)
  • Expected return (consumption growth rate)
  • Payback period per engagement type
  • Resource allocation framework

Comp Architecture

  • SEs: influence on consumption growth, not just bookings
  • Post-Sales: workload conversion rate + consumption ramp
  • PS: utilization + customer outcomes (not just billable hours)
  • Support: resolution quality + deflection rate

Goal Setting

  • Team-level goals tied to shared metrics (NRR, TTFV, CES)
  • Individual goals tied to function-specific KPIs
  • Cross-functional bonus triggers (handoff quality)
  • Consumption forecasts as leading indicators

Snowflake

Pioneered consumption-based comp for field teams. Reps compensated on consumption growth, not bookings. This eliminated the “shelf-ware” problem where deals close but never activate. PS charged at cost to align with adoption, not revenue extraction.

Databricks

Hybrid model: bookings component + consumption kicker. SEs had a dual metric: technical win rate + influenced consumption growth at 6 months post-close. This forced SEs to care about implementation success, not just closing.

Twilio

Developer evangelism comp tied to activation metrics (first API call within 7 days). Account teams comped on usage growth, not seat expansion. Clear separation between “land” (new logo) and “expand” (consumption) comp plans.

What VP RevOps Owns Here

  • Comp plan design — structure, targets, accelerators aligned to consumption, not just bookings
  • Quota methodology — bottoms-up capacity models, not top-down revenue targets divided by headcount
  • Investment analysis — which accounts justify dedicated coverage, what's the ROI on PS engagements
  • Goal alignment — ensure team-level goals don't conflict (SE closes deal that PS can't implement)

Vercel Gap

David explicitly said: “Moving to consumption-based comp.” But can't track consumption curves yet. No infrastructure to tie comp to consumption outcomes. Current strategy ops is execution-focused, not designing these frameworks.

The team that compounds expertise across the org. Every SE engagement, every PS implementation, every Support escalation generates knowledge. Without STS, that knowledge stays in one person's head. With STS, it becomes a reusable asset.

  • Product/Eng interlock built into the FE org

Why It Accelerates Revenue

  • PS implements faster when patterns are documented and shared
  • Support resolves faster when escalation paths and context exist
  • Product ships better when field signals are structured, not anecdotal
At Databricks, SAs were verticalized and made commercial contributors — they identified new workloads, architected solutions, and directly drove expansion. STS is the function that enables this at scale.

No Charter, No Scope, No Metrics

“There's not a lot of us out there.”

The team exists but has no defined operating model. No engagement triggers from the other 4 customer-facing teams. No way to measure impact. People don't know when to pull STS in or what to expect when they do.

No Vertical Specialization

Vercel's customer base spans e-commerce, SaaS platforms, media/content, AI applications, and enterprise portals. Each has radically different architecture needs, deployment patterns, and buying motions. Today, every SE and PS engineer is a generalist. No one goes deep on any vertical.

Product/Eng Interlock Doesn't Exist

Field teams surface platform gaps every day — missing features, edge cases, performance limits. None of this reaches Product in a structured way. David built this interlock at Databricks. It doesn't exist at Vercel yet.

Territory Was 1 Rep for All Software Companies

Until Feb 1st: zero segmentation, zero prioritization.

E-commerce accounts already on Vercel had no dedicated coverage. No capacity model. No framework for deciding which accounts justify deep technical investment vs. scaled coverage.

STS should own four things. Each one directly accelerates revenue.

1. Vertical Specialization — Use-Case Expertise

Databricks verticalized by industry (FSI, Retail, Healthcare). Vercel should verticalize by use case — it maps better to how the platform is actually used:

  • E-Commerce & Storefronts — headless commerce, Shopify integrations, performance-critical catalog pages. Largest existing enterprise base
  • SaaS Platforms — multi-tenant architectures, API-heavy apps, CI/CD pipeline optimization
  • AI Applications — v0 integration, AI SDK usage, streaming architectures, compute-intensive workloads. Fastest-growing segment
  • Content & Media — CMS-driven sites, ISR patterns, global edge delivery, high-traffic events

Revenue impact: SEs with vertical expertise win more deals (they speak the customer's language), PS implements faster (reusable patterns per use case), Support resolves faster (known issues per architecture type).

2. Product/Engineering Interlock

The structured bridge between field signals and product roadmap. This is what David built at Databricks and explicitly said is missing at Vercel.

  • Monthly field intelligence brief — top 10 platform friction points, weighted by ARR impact
  • Quarterly roadmap alignment — STS architects review upcoming features against real customer use cases
  • Closed-loop tracking — field teams see when their feedback ships (maintains trust and keeps the feedback coming)
  • Early access program — STS tests new features with strategic accounts before GA, catching issues before they hit Support

The Operating Model

RevOps doesn't run the teams. It builds the operating system underneath them so they function as one unit.

The Big Idea

Handoffs are the product. They're not an administrative step between teams — they're a customer experience that directly impacts NRR. Measure them, design them, optimize them like you would any product. The operating system isn't Salesforce or Gainsight. It's the rhythm, the data model, the accountability framework, and the shared metrics that make five teams feel like one to the customer.

Handoff Protocol — "The Baton"

Every transition between teams follows a structured protocol. No cold handoffs. No lost context.

What Transfers

  • Customer context — who they are, what they bought, why
  • Success criteria — what "success" looks like in the customer's words
  • Technical environment — stack, integrations, constraints
  • Key contacts & politics — decision maker, champion, detractor, technical lead
  • Open items — unresolved questions, known risks, commitments made
  • Timeline — critical dates, go-live targets, renewal date

How It Executes

  • Warm intro call — never a cold email. Outgoing team makes the introduction live
  • 2-week overlap — both teams are active during transition
  • Customer survey — handoff quality measured 1 week after transition
  • Completion tracking — handoff completion rate is a team-level KPI
  • Automated triggers — system-enforced steps, not doc-driven process
  • Feedback loop — monthly review of handoff quality scores

Devil's Advocate: Why Not CSAT?

CSAT seems like the obvious satisfaction metric, but it's unreliable for enterprise Field Engineering. Response rates are 5-15% — biased toward extremes (angry or delighted). In multi-threaded enterprise relationships, "whose satisfaction?" is ambiguous — the developer using the product daily has a different experience than the VP who signed the contract. And CSAT measures a moment, not the journey.

Use CES (Customer Effort Score) instead as the north star. CES measures friction — how hard it is to get help, complete a handoff, resolve an issue. That's exactly what this operating model is designed to reduce. CSAT still works at the interaction level (per support ticket, per PS engagement) — but not as an account-level health indicator.
